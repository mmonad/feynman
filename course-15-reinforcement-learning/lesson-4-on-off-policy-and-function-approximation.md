## Core Question

What does it actually mean for an algorithm to learn "on-policy" vs "off-policy," why does this distinction matter so much for stability and sample efficiency, and what goes wrong when you combine function approximation with the wrong ingredients?

## Q57: Off-Policy vs On-Policy

### The Setup

Every RL algorithm generates data by having some agent interact with the environment. The **behavioral policy** μ is the policy that actually picks actions and generates transitions. The **target policy** π is the policy you're trying to learn about (evaluate or improve).

The distinction is simple:

- **On-policy:** μ = π. You learn about the policy you're currently running.
- **Off-policy:** μ ≠ π. You learn about a different policy from the data generated by μ.

That's it. But the consequences are enormous.

### On-Policy: SARSA, A2C, PPO

In SARSA, you update Q based on the action your current policy actually took:

```
Q(s, a) ← Q(s, a) + α [ r + γ Q(s', a') - Q(s, a) ]
```

where a' is the action your policy actually selected in state s' (hence S-A-R-S-A). You're evaluating and improving the policy that generated the data.

**Consequence:** Every time you update the policy (even slightly), all your old data was generated by the old policy. It's no longer representative of what the new policy would do. You must throw it away and collect fresh data. This is **sample inefficient** — you burn through enormous amounts of environment interaction.

**Advantage:** Stability. Since your data matches your current policy, the updates are well-behaved. You're optimizing the thing you're measuring.

PPO is on-policy with a clever trick: it reuses each batch of data for multiple gradient steps, but clips the objective so the policy can't change too much from the data-generating policy. It squeezes more updates out of each data batch without going fully off-policy.

### Off-Policy: Q-Learning, DQN, SAC

In Q-learning, you update Q based on the best action available, regardless of what you actually did:

```
Q(s, a) ← Q(s, a) + α [ r + γ max_{a'} Q(s', a') - Q(s, a) ]
```

That `max` means you're learning about the greedy policy (always pick the best action), even if the behavioral policy was ε-greedy or something else entirely. You can store transitions from any behavioral policy in a replay buffer and learn from them later.

**Consequence:** Massive sample efficiency. Old transitions are still useful — the Bellman equation Q*(s,a) = R + γ Σ P max Q*(s',a') holds regardless of which policy generated the data. One transition can be reused hundreds of times.

**Problem:** The data distribution doesn't match the target policy's distribution. Actions that π would frequently take might be rare in μ's data, and vice versa. This **distribution mismatch** introduces errors that compound through bootstrapping.

### Importance Sampling: Correcting the Mismatch

When you want to evaluate a target policy π using data from behavioral policy μ, you can reweight the returns:

```
ρ_t = π(a_t | s_t) / μ(a_t | s_t)
```

The importance sampling ratio ρ_t corrects for the fact that action a_t was chosen with probability μ(a_t|s_t) but should have been chosen with probability π(a_t|s_t). For a trajectory of length T, the cumulative ratio is:

```
ρ_{0:T} = Π_{t=0}^{T} π(a_t|s_t) / μ(a_t|s_t)
```

The corrected return estimate is E_μ[ρ_{0:T} · G] = E_π[G]. Unbiased! But look at that product. If T is large, you're multiplying many ratios together. Even if each ratio is reasonable, the product has **exponentially high variance**. A single trajectory where π and μ disagree on even a few actions can produce a ρ of 10^6 or 10^-6, making the estimator useless.

This is why practical off-policy methods like DQN and SAC avoid importance sampling entirely. Q-learning sidesteps it by using the `max` operator directly. SAC uses an entropy-regularized framework. Only when you specifically need to evaluate a policy from off-policy data (like in the evaluation step of policy optimization) do you grudgingly use importance sampling — and you truncate or clip the ratios to manage variance.

### The Practical Landscape

| Aspect | On-Policy (PPO, A2C) | Off-Policy (DQN, SAC) |
|---|---|---|
| **Data reuse** | Low (discard after each update) | High (replay buffer) |
| **Sample efficiency** | Poor | Good |
| **Stability** | High (data matches policy) | Lower (distribution mismatch) |
| **Wall-clock efficiency** | Often faster despite more samples (simpler updates) | Slower per update, fewer updates needed |
| **Continuous actions** | PPO handles well | SAC is the gold standard |
| **Use in RLHF** | PPO (on-policy, easy to keep policy near reference) | Emerging (DPO avoids RL entirely) |

### Why PPO Is "On-Policy with Tricks"

PPO collects a batch of trajectories, then performs multiple epochs of gradient updates on that batch. Strictly speaking, after the first gradient step, the policy has changed — so the data is slightly off-policy. The clipped surrogate objective limits how far the policy can drift:

```
L^CLIP(θ) = E[ min(ρ_t · A_t, clip(ρ_t, 1-ε, 1+ε) · A_t) ]
```

where ρ_t = π_θ(a_t|s_t) / π_{θ_old}(a_t|s_t). If the policy tries to change too much (ρ far from 1), the objective is clipped. This keeps PPO in the "slightly off-policy" regime where it works well.

### Why SAC Is "Off-Policy Done Right"

SAC (Soft Actor-Critic) maximizes reward plus entropy:

```
J(π) = E[ Σ γ^t (r_t + α H(π(·|s_t))) ]
```

The entropy bonus H encourages exploration automatically — no need for ε-greedy. SAC uses a replay buffer (off-policy), twin Q-networks (to combat overestimation), and targets the soft Bellman equation. It's remarkably stable for an off-policy method because the entropy regularization smooths the optimization landscape.

> **Key takeaway:** On-policy is stable but wasteful; off-policy is efficient but treacherous. The importance sampling ratio is the theoretical bridge between them, but its variance makes it impractical for long horizons. Modern algorithms (PPO, SAC) position themselves carefully on this spectrum — PPO stays close to on-policy with clipping, SAC tames off-policy learning through entropy regularization.

## Q58: Function Approximation Issues in RL

### The Convergence Hierarchy

Let's be honest about what we can prove:

**Tabular RL:** Full convergence guarantees. Q-learning converges to Q* (Watkins and Dayan, 1992). SARSA converges to Q^π. TD(0) converges to V^π. These are theorems with proofs. The catch: you need every state-action pair visited infinitely often. In large state spaces, this is impossible.

**Linear function approximation:** Some guarantees, with caveats. If you represent V as V(s) = θ^T φ(s) for fixed features φ, then:
- On-policy TD(0) converges (Tsitsiklis and Van Roy) — but to a biased solution, the "TD fixed point," not the true V^π
- Off-policy TD(0) can diverge — even linear + off-policy + bootstrapping is deadly
- LSTD (Least-Squares TD) converges to the TD fixed point in one shot given enough data

**Neural function approximation:** No convergence guarantees whatsoever. None. The universal approximation theorem tells you the network can represent the right answer, but no theorem tells you that gradient descent on the TD loss will find it. We rely entirely on empirical evidence and engineering.

### The Deadly Triad, Precisely

Let's revisit this with more precision. The three ingredients:

1. **Function approximation:** The value function generalizes across states. Updating V(s) changes V(s') for similar states. In a table, updating one entry leaves all others untouched. With a neural net, changing one prediction changes many.

2. **Bootstrapping:** The learning target includes the model's own predictions. The TD target `r + γ V(s')` uses V(s'), which is itself an estimate. This creates a feedback loop: errors in V(s') contaminate the target for V(s), which in turn contaminates future targets.

3. **Off-policy learning:** Data comes from a distribution that doesn't match the target policy's stationary distribution. The function approximator tries to fit a moving, mismatched data stream.

### Why Any Two Are Fine

**FA + Bootstrapping (on-policy):** This is on-policy TD with a neural net. The data distribution matches the target policy. The updates can be seen as semi-gradient descent on a well-defined objective (the mean squared projected Bellman error, at least in the linear case). In practice, this works — A2C, PPO.

**FA + Off-policy (no bootstrapping):** This is off-policy Monte Carlo. You use importance sampling to reweight full returns, then regress V or Q on those returns. The targets are fixed quantities (actual returns, not estimates), so you're doing standard supervised learning with reweighted samples. High variance from importance ratios, but no divergence.

**Bootstrapping + Off-policy (tabular):** This is tabular Q-learning. Off-policy and bootstrapping, but no function approximation. Each Q(s,a) is an independent entry. Updating Q(s,a) has zero effect on Q(s',a'). The feedback loop can't propagate through shared parameters. Convergence proved.

### Baird's Counterexample

This is the canonical demonstration that the triad diverges. Baird (1995) constructed a simple 7-state MDP with linear function approximation where off-policy TD(0) diverges — the parameter vector θ grows without bound, oscillating wildly.

The setup: the behavioral policy visits all states uniformly, but the target policy concentrates on a single state. With a carefully chosen (but perfectly reasonable) feature representation, the TD updates push θ away from the solution at every step. The projection of the Bellman operator onto the feature space is an expansion, not a contraction.

This isn't a pathological edge case engineered to break things — it's a simple, natural setup that happens to have misaligned features and policy distributions.

### Why Deep RL Works Despite All This

Given the lack of theoretical guarantees, why does DQN play Atari? Why does SAC control robots? Engineering:

| Trick | What It Addresses |
|---|---|
| **Experience replay** | Breaks correlation, provides pseudo-i.i.d. data |
| **Target networks** | Stabilizes bootstrapping targets |
| **Gradient clipping** | Prevents catastrophically large updates |
| **Layer normalization** | Stabilizes internal representations |
| **Double Q-learning** | Reduces maximization bias |
| **Soft (entropy-regularized) objectives** | Smooths the optimization landscape |
| **Conservative Q-learning** | Penalizes OOD state-action values (offline RL) |

Each of these is an engineering patch for a specific failure mode. Together, they create conditions where the deadly triad's pathologies are suppressed — not eliminated, but pushed to rare edge cases that don't arise in practice for the environments we care about.

The honest interview answer: "Deep RL works in practice because a suite of stabilization tricks collectively prevent divergence, even though no individual trick provides a convergence guarantee. We're in the regime of empirical machine learning, not provable algorithms."

> **Key takeaway:** The deadly triad is real and proven. Any two of {function approximation, bootstrapping, off-policy} are safe; all three can diverge. Deep RL has all three and works anyway, but only because of a stack of engineering countermeasures. An interviewer asking about this wants to hear that you understand both the theory (it shouldn't work) and the practice (it does, and here's why).

## Q&A

**Question:** In RLHF with PPO, is the training on-policy or off-policy? The model generates responses, gets a reward from the reward model, and then updates. But the batch is reused for multiple gradient steps.

**Student's Answer:** "It's on-policy with a small off-policy correction. The model generates a batch of responses using the current policy π_θ_old. Then PPO does multiple epochs of gradient steps on that batch. After the first gradient step, θ has changed, so technically the data was generated by π_θ_old, not π_θ — which is off-policy. The clipped surrogate objective limits how far π_θ can drift from π_θ_old, keeping the effective importance ratio ρ = π_θ/π_θ_old close to 1. There's also typically a KL penalty between π_θ and the reference (SFT) policy to prevent reward hacking. So it's 'on-policy with a controlled amount of off-policy drift, bounded by clipping.'"

**Evaluation:** Precisely right. The student correctly identified that PPO in RLHF is not purely on-policy (the data is reused), but the clipping mechanism keeps it close enough that the theoretical on-policy assumptions approximately hold. The mention of the KL penalty against the SFT reference model shows understanding of the full RLHF pipeline, not just the RL component.

**Question:** An interviewer shows you Baird's counterexample and asks: "What would you change to make it converge, while keeping function approximation and off-policy learning?"

**Student's Answer:** "Remove bootstrapping. Use off-policy Monte Carlo — compute full returns from the trajectories, reweight by importance sampling ratios, and train the linear function approximator to regress on those reweighted returns. The targets are now fixed (actual returns, not estimates), so you're doing standard supervised regression. The off-policy importance weights add variance, but there's no divergence. Alternatively, keep all three but use gradient-TD methods like GTD2 or TDC, which are designed to converge under the deadly triad by using a different (convergent) update rule instead of standard semi-gradient TD."

**Evaluation:** Both solutions are correct. The first (remove bootstrapping) is the textbook answer. The second (gradient-TD methods) is the research-level answer — GTD2 converges under the deadly triad by performing true gradient descent on the mean-squared projected Bellman error, rather than the semi-gradient approach of standard TD. Mentioning both shows depth.
